Condition 1 — EMA 9/21 crossover
Valid
Correct EMA pairing for 15-min NSE intraday
9/21 EMA is a well-tested pairing for this timeframe. Fast enough to catch intraday momentum, slow enough to filter noise. Used widely by prop desks in Indian equities. Veteran note: the "pullback to 21 EMA" variant is slightly subjecive — define it strictly as "price closes between 9 EMA and 21 EMA, 21 EMA is holding as support."
Gap
EMA slope is not checked
A crossover on a flat or declining EMA is much weaker than one where both EMAs are angled in the direction of the trade. Add: for a Long, both 9 EMA and 21 EMA should be sloping upward (current value > value 2 bars ago). Filters ~20% of false signals.
Condition 2 — Pivot level confirmation
Valid
Classic pivot point methodology — correct formulas
Standard floor pivot formulas (P = H+L+C ÷ 3) are correct. These are the most widely watched levels in NSE equities. Institutional algorithms and large prop desks use these exact levels, which is precisely why they work as support/resistance.
Gap
No buffer zone around pivot levels
Price will frequently close 2–4 paise above a pivot level and then reverse. The system has no tolerance band. Add: require candle to close at least 0.1% (10 bps) beyond the pivot level — not just touching it. This removes many fakeout entries.
Condition 3 — ADX(14) trend strength
Valid
ADX(14) > 20 with DI check is professionally sound
This is the most important filter in the system. Approximately 60–65% of NSE intraday sessions are rangebound; ADX filters these out. The +DI/-DI directional check is correctly included. This single condition, if respected strictly, will save more capital than any other rule.
Valid
ADX exit trigger (drop below 18) is a professional touch
Exiting when ADX drops below 18 mid-trade is correct and often missed by retail systems. A fading ADX means the trend is dying and the trade's thesis is invalidated — even if price hasn't hit your stop yet. Good rule.
Condition 4 — Volume spike (≥1.5× 20-bar MA)
Valid
Volume confirmation is essential — correctly applied
The 1.5× threshold is appropriate for Nifty 50 large-caps. These stocks average 8–15 crore shares/day; a 1.5× spike reliably signals institutional participation vs retail noise. The 20-bar MA baseline is correct — it adapts to intraday volume patterns automatically.
Gap
INFY and TCS volume sensitivity differs from banking stocks
IT stocks (INFY, TCS) have structurally lower intraday volume than HDFCBANK/ICICIBANK/RELIANCE. A 1.5× spike on INFY carries different significance than the same ratio on RELIANCE. Consider: raising threshold to 1.8× for IT stocks to ensure genuine institutional participation.
Position sizing — 1–2% risk per trade
Excellent
1–2% risk per trade is the industry standard for new traders
This is exactly what any professional risk manager would prescribe for a beginner with a ₹2L account. At 2% risk, you can take 50 consecutive maximum-loss trades before account blowup — enough runway to learn from mistakes. Most retail traders fail using 5–10% risk per trade.
Valid
3% daily loss limit is correctly calibrated
A 3% daily hard stop is tight but appropriate for beginners. Experienced traders may use 5%, but 3% forces session discipline and prevents the common "revenge trading spiral" where a bad morning leads to a catastrophic afternoon. Correct rule.
Valid
60% T1 / 40% T2 partial exit is sound
Booking 60% at T1 (R1/S1) and moving stop to breakeven on remainder is a proven approach. It locks in real profit on the majority of the position while giving the remaining 40% free room to run. The breakeven stop on remainder eliminates the psychological pressure of "giving back profits."
Issue
Max stop = 0.5% of stock price can conflict with pivot-based SL
RELIANCE pivot range (R1–S1) can span ₹40–70 on a volatile day, making the natural SL 1.2–2% from entry — far exceeding the 0.5% hard cap. When the pivot-based SL exceeds 0.5%, skip the trade entirely. The system needs this stated explicitly as a trade filter, not just a SL cap.
Missing
No transaction cost model — this is critical for P&L realism
At ₹2L capital with 1–2% risk trades, brokerage + STT + SEBI charges + GST will cost approximately ₹80–150 per round trip on large-cap stocks. On a trade where T1 yields ₹23/share × 20 shares = ₹460 gross, costs eat ~25–33% of the gain. The stated 1:1.8 R:R is gross, not net. Net R:R after costs is closer to 1:1.3 on small positions.

Realistic
15–25 trades/month is achievable with 5-stock universe
With strict 4-condition filtering, expect 1–2 valid setups per stock per week. Across 5 stocks, 15–25/month is realistic. The 3-trade/day limit further constrains this. This frequency is actually healthy — enough data to learn from, low enough to avoid overtrading fees and decision fatigue.
These are not cosmetic issues. Each gap below will materially affect real-money performance. Treat these as prerequisites before live trading.
Gap 1 — No higher timeframe (HTF) bias filter
Critical
Trading 15-min signals against the daily/hourly trend will destroy win rate
The system allows both Long and Short trades at any time, purely based on 15-min conditions. In practice, taking a Long setup when the stock has been in a clear daily downtrend, or when the Nifty 50 is falling 1.5% on the day, dramatically lowers probability. A system without an HTF bias filter is like driving with one eye closed. A 30-min or daily chart check before any entry should be mandatory — never trade against the larger structure.
Gap 2 — No Nifty 50 market-wide filter
Critical
Individual stock setups during market-wide panic/euphoria have low reliability
If Nifty 50 is down >1% on the day, a LONG signal on RELIANCE has significantly lower probability of succeeding than on a flat or bullish market day. If Nifty is up >1%, LONG signals have a higher success rate. The system mentions "check Gift Nifty" but does not translate that into a concrete trading rule. Missing rule: If Nifty 50 moves >1.5% from previous close in either direction by 10 AM, reduce all position sizes by 50% for the day.
Gap 3 — Transaction costs are absent from the performance model
Critical
Advertised 1:1.8 R:R does not account for NSE trading costs
Every NSE intraday trade incurs: Zerodha brokerage (₹20/order = ₹40 round trip), STT on sells (0.025% of turnover), SEBI fees (0.0001%), Exchange charges, GST. On a ₹1,500 gross-profit trade with a small position, total costs can be ₹120–200. Over 20 trades/month, this is ₹2,400–4,000/month in frictional costs. The minimum net R:R required to be profitable after costs, at 55% win rate, is approximately 1:1.4 — not 1:1.8 gross.
Gap 4 — Shorting mechanics for NSE are incompletely addressed
Important
Intraday short selling on NSE cash segment has unique risks
The system mentions "MIS margin in Zerodha" for shorts but does not address: (1) Not all stocks support intraday short selling with MIS at all times — brokerage can block MIS on volatile days. (2) Short positions must be squared off by 3:20 PM or the broker does it automatically, often at unfavorable prices. (3) Short setups on ex-dividend days carry gap risk. For beginners, restrict to LONG trades only for the first 3 months. Add shorts only after achieving consistent results on longs.
Gap 5 — Win rate and R:R claims are theoretical, not backtested
Important
Stated 52–58% win rate and 1:1.8 R:R are estimates, not verified figures
These are reasonable estimates for this type of multi-condition system on large-cap NSE stocks — but they are not validated against historical data. Markets change. In trending months (Oct–Nov 2023, Jan–Feb 2024) this system would likely outperform the stated range. In choppy months (Jul–Aug 2024) it would underperform. Before live trading, manually backtest this system on at least 60 historical trading days across at least 2 of the 5 stocks. Look specifically at months with mixed market conditions.
Implement all 5 fixes below before trading real money. Each is specific and actionable. These upgrades take the system from 7.1 to ~8.5.
Fix 1 — Add a 5-rule HTF bias check (takes 2 minutes each morning)
Before any trade, open the daily chart of the stock. Rule: If the stock's last 3 daily candles are all red and price is below the 20-day EMA, mark it as "bearish bias today — Longs need extra caution, Shorts preferred." If last 3 candles are green and above 20D EMA, mark as "bullish bias — Longs preferred, Shorts need extra caution." Only take setups that align with the daily bias. This simple filter is expected to increase win rate by 5–8 percentage points.
Fix 2 — Add Nifty 50 market filter to your pre-market checklist
Add this single rule to your Section 05 daily checklist: "Check Nifty 50 at 10:00 AM. If Nifty is down more than 1.5%, only take SHORT setups and reduce all position sizes by 50%. If Nifty is up more than 1.5%, only take LONG setups and reduce position sizes by 50% (volatility warning)." On moderate market days (Nifty within ±1%), trade normally in both directions.
Fix 3 — Build a real cost-adjusted profit target
For every trade, calculate your true breakeven: Entry cost + Exit cost + STT + Charges ≈ ₹150–200 per round trip. Your T1 target (R1 or S1) must yield at least ₹500 gross on 60% of position to achieve ₹300 net after costs. If the distance from entry to T1 is too small to generate ₹500 gross on your position size, skip the trade — it is not worth the friction. Add this as a 5th pre-trade check.
Fix 4 — Restrict to Longs-only for first 90 days
Indian cash equities have an upward structural drift. Long setups on large-cap NSE stocks in a trending market work better than shorts. More importantly, short selling mechanics (MIS margin blocks, forced broker squarings, auction penalties for short delivery) are a trap for beginners. Trade only Long setups for the first 3 months. Add shorts to your system only after you have 30+ documented Long trades with at least 50% win rate and positive net profit factor.
Fix 5 — Add EMA slope filter to Condition 1
Modify Condition 1 as follows: For a Long setup, require that the 21 EMA value is higher now than it was 2 candles ago (i.e., 21 EMA is rising). For a Short setup, require 21 EMA is lower than 2 candles ago. A crossover on a flat EMA produces the most false signals. This micro-filter is easy to eyeball on a chart and prevents entering into choppy crossovers. In Python automation, this is a single line: ema21.iloc[-1] > ema21.iloc[-3].
